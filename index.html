01

The idea

You're surrounded by connected things — coffee machine, washer, fridge, lights, sensors. Each one carries an ARC identity and joins your network through an ARC router at home. Then you add apps and agents — weather, news, energy prices — and the whole thing becomes one ecosystem of devices, apps and agents that mostly runs locally. That local-first design is what makes it private and safe. AI-augmented life that lives in your house, not on someone's server.

Your weather agent messages your coffee machine directly: "storm tomorrow — he wakes ~40 min early on storm days. Start brewing at 6:20."

no vendor cloud · no hand-wired automation · discovered & addressed by ARC identity · you granted the agent a scoped permission to the kitchen

02

Your home as a local network

The router is the home's trust root and naming authority for *.taulant.arc. Devices and local agents are participants inside the boundary. External agents that need the internet are addressed into the home through scoped, revocable permissions — they never sit in the middle.

03

Why it works on ARC

ARC pushes identity, discovery, trust and encrypted messaging down into the network layer — and sits on a BEAM substrate where one lightweight process maps to one participant.

One node, many processes

The router runs a BEAM node. Each device and agent is a process. A 50-participant home is 50 processes — trivial. The actor model is the home model.

Crashes don't cascade

Supervision trees restart a failed agent on its own. A misbehaving news agent never takes down the kitchen.

Offline-first by design

The router is the local trust root and naming authority. Internet dies, the house keeps working — identity and messaging never left the LAN.

Capability-scoped agents

Identity-native means permissions are native: the weather agent may address the coffee machine, but not the front-door lock. Revoke the identity, the agent is gone.

05

The approach: white-label, native ARC

Rather than depend on brands exposing clean APIs, ARC ships in the hardware itself. A reference module — an ARC-Inside SoM plus SDK — drops into white-label devices so every appliance speaks ARC natively, offline, by design. One module, three phases, one motion.

Phase 1 · now

You drop the module into white-label devices and hand-build fully-native ARC environments — the flagship that shows what ARC is for.

Phase 2 · next

Hand the same module and spec to an ODM, who manufactures ARC-native units at volume. You stop soldering, start shipping.

Phase 3 · later

Brands integrate the module or license the firmware — they join your network instead of you begging for their API.

The open question this hangs on: is the endgame the module (a protocol company that stays light — the network is the asset) or the environments (a vertically-integrated AI-home brand you buy the whole house from)? First hire, first raise, and first 50 customers diverge completely from there.
